How does this work at the moment?

In most large organisations, this process is carried out manually. Staff shuffle big piles of paper and try to remember what they've seen and if it would be suitable. It's a lot of effort, and never - despite best efforts - seems to work out particularly well. There's a really good reason for this: it's almost impossible to do manually.

Often, role templates are written like job adverts. Matchers have to parse complex technical language to understand what's being offered. Then they try to decide which of the candidates they've seen would be well suited. This approach can be open to bias, as certain candidates might stick in the mind more than others. It also lacks transparency and objectivity. A different matcher might make a different decision, and it's often done on instinct or gut feeling rather than anything quantifiable.

In addition, because these templates are put together in word-processing software, they're not optimised to be viewed on the internet. The data within them is also locked away. As a senior leader, it's vital that you know what people on your development schemes are being asked to do. These requests often indicate critical gaps in your organisation's capability - but even the most committed senior leader might hesitate at reviewing three or four hundred pages of prose.

The approach I've taken with this software is that the role profile is for the candidate to read: it should give them a strong flavour of what they'll be doing. However, the information that's important to the matcher is whether it will stretch the individual. I've quantified this with scales of 0-5.

I've also chosen an algorithm that optimises for the best overall outcome. In the next few pages I'll take you through a worked example, as well as explaining why I think this problem is much harder than it first appears.
